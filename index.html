Module Overview

What This Module Builds

Client objections are not always a rejection. Sometimes they are a request for more clarity, more trust, more evidence, or a better reason to move forward.

In this module

We’ll practise how to respond when a client questions price, timing, value, urgency, fit, or next steps.

The goal is not to memorise a perfect script. The goal is to build a reliable way to handle pushback while staying professional and in control.

Skill Path

Objection handling is built one layer at a time.

A strong objection response is not just one sentence. It is a sequence — stay calm, understand what the client really means, respond clearly, and keep the conversation moving.

1
Slow the moment down
Create space before answering.
2
Acknowledge without surrendering
Show that you understand the concern without giving up your position.
3
Clarify the real concern
Find out what the objection actually means before answering it.
4
Reframe the issue
Shift the conversation from resistance to value, fit, timing, priority, or business impact.
5
Respond with value
Connect your response back to the client’s problem, desired result, or cost of inaction.
6
Move the conversation forward
Do not let the objection become the end of the conversation.

Core Framework

The Objection Response Framework

When a client pushes back, it is easy to react too quickly. This structure gives you a clear path.

1
Acknowledge
Show that you understand the concern.
“I understand why that would be a concern.”
2
Clarify
Ask what the real issue is before answering.
“Can I clarify whether the concern is budget, timing, or expected return?”
3
Reframe
Move the conversation toward the bigger issue.
“The bigger question is whether this solves the problem you described.”
4
Respond with Value
Connect your answer to the client’s problem or desired result.
“The value here is that your team can reduce delays and avoid manual follow-up.”
5
Move Forward
Create a useful next step.
“Would it make sense to compare this with your current process?”
Framework note
You do not need to use every step every time. The framework gives you a path to follow when the conversation becomes difficult.

Mini Example — Live Conversation
Client
“It’s too expensive.”
You Acknowledge + Clarify
“I understand why price matters. Can I clarify whether the concern is the total cost, or whether the value is not clear enough yet?”
Client
“Mostly the value. I’m not sure the benefit justifies the cost.”
You Reframe
“That makes sense. Then the useful place to focus is the outcome, not only the price.”
Client
“Okay, what outcome are you referring to?”
You Respond + Move Forward
“If the main issue is lost time, the value is that your team can reduce delays and work more consistently. Would it make sense to compare this with your current process so you can judge whether the value is strong enough?”

Situation Map

Common Types of Pushback

Most objections point to a concern we need to understand. The basic response path stays the same — the language adapts to the situation.

Tap a card to expand it.

Price Concern
“It’s too expensive.”
Usually about: value, budget, comparison, or confidence in the result.
The client may not be saying the price is absolutely too high — they may be saying the value is not yet clear enough to justify the cost.
First move: clarify what “expensive” means
Timing Concern
“Now isn’t the right time.”
Usually about: priority, urgency, workload, or internal process.
Timing objections often mask a priority concern. The question is whether the client sees this as important enough to act on now.
First move: ask what would make it a better time
Existing Provider
“We already use another provider.”
Usually about: comfort, switching cost, or unclear difference.
The goal is not to attack their current provider — it is to understand whether the current setup fully solves their problem.
First move: ask what is working and what is still missing
Send Me Information
“Can you just send me more information?”
Usually about: low commitment, unclear value, or avoiding a decision.
This is often a polite way to end the conversation. Clarify what information would actually help, not just agree to send something generic.
First move: clarify what information would actually help
Need to Think
“I need to think about it.”
Usually about: hidden concern, uncertainty, or unclear decision criteria.
There is almost always a specific reason behind this. The goal is to make it easier for the client to name it by asking gently.
First move: ask what they need to think through
Not Worth It
“I’m not sure it’s worth it.”
Usually about: unclear value, weak impact, or lack of proof.
The client may not yet see a clear connection between your solution and a meaningful result for them. Make that connection explicit.
First move: connect the solution to the business result
Internal Approval
“My manager/team may not agree.”
Usually about: stakeholder risk or internal justification.
The client may want to move forward but is worried about internal resistance. Help them think through what their manager would need to see.
First move: ask what the team would need to see

Skill 1 of 6

Slow the Moment Down

Key Move: Create space before answering.
Why this matters
When a client pushes back, many speakers respond too quickly. They defend, over-explain, apologise, or offer solutions before they understand the concern. A short pause and a calm opening phrase help you stay in control before you respond.
You do not need to answer immediately to sound confident.
Useful language
“That’s a fair question.”
“I understand why that matters.”
“Let me respond to that clearly.”
“Before I answer directly, can I clarify one thing?”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Yes, sorry, I understand. Maybe we can reduce the price.”
Stronger
“I understand the concern. Before we talk about price, it may help to look at what is included and what problem we’re trying to solve.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“This seems expensive.”
Model response
“I hear you — the price feels high. That’s a fair concern. Let’s step back for a second and look at what’s driving that.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
“It’s too expensive.”
Task: Respond with one calm opening phrase. Do not defend, apologise, discount, or explain yet.
“This seems expensive compared with other options.”
Task: Start with a calm control phrase before giving any explanation.
“Look, I just don’t think the price makes sense.”
Challenge: The client sounds impatient. Try again with a calm opening that does not escalate.
Quick Recap
A strong response often starts by slowing the moment down.
“Before I answer directly, can I clarify one thing?”
Skill 2 of 6

Acknowledge Without Surrendering

Key Move: Show that you understand the concern without giving up your position.
Why this matters
If you skip acknowledgement, the client may feel ignored, even if your answer is technically good. Acknowledging first shows that you heard the concern before you respond.
You can understand the concern without agreeing that your offer is wrong.
Useful language
“I understand why that would be a concern.”
“That’s a reasonable point to raise.”
“I can see why you’d ask that.”
“That makes sense. Let’s look at it properly.”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Yes, I know it’s expensive. Sorry about that.”
Stronger
“I understand why the price matters. The important question is whether the value matches the problem you’re trying to solve.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“I’m not sure this is the right time for us.”
Model response
“That makes sense. Timing is important, especially if your team already has a lot moving right now.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
“Your solution is more expensive than the others.”
Task: Acknowledge the objection without apologising.
“I’m not convinced this is worth the investment.”
Task: Acknowledge the concern while keeping your position stable.
“Honestly, I expected it to be cheaper.”
Challenge: The client adds this after their first objection. Acknowledge without over-apologising.
Quick Recap
Acknowledgement is not surrender.
“I understand why that would be a concern.”
Skill 3 of 6

Clarify the Real Concern

Key Move: Change the focus from the objection itself to the result the client is trying to achieve.
Why this matters
Many objections are unclear at first. “It’s too expensive” could mean budget, unclear value, comparison with another option, weak timing, or low confidence in the result. If you respond too quickly, you may answer the wrong objection.
Clarify before you defend.
Useful language
“Can I clarify one thing first?”
“Is the main concern budget, timing, or value?”
“Are you comparing this with another option?”
“What would need to be clearer for this to feel worthwhile?”
“Is it more about cost, or confidence in the result?”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“But our product is very good and we have many features.”
Stronger
“Can I clarify whether the concern is the total price, or whether the value is not clear enough yet?”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“It’s too expensive.”
Model response
“Can I clarify whether the concern is the total price, or whether the value is not clear enough yet?”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
“It’s too expensive.”
Task: Turn the objection into a clarification question.
“Is the concern the total price or the return you expect?”
“Are you comparing this with another option?”
“Is it more about budget or confidence in the result?”
“I don’t know if this is the right time.”
Task: Ask one question to understand the real timing concern.
“I don’t know. It just feels like a lot right now.”
Challenge: The client gives a vague answer. Ask one gentle clarifying question that moves the conversation forward.
Quick Recap
Good objection handling often starts with a question, not an answer.
“Can I clarify one thing first?”
Skill 4 of 6

Reframe the Objection

Key Move: Change the focus from the objection itself to the result the client is trying to achieve.
Why this matters
If you stay trapped inside the objection, the conversation can become defensive. A reframe moves the focus back to value, fit, timing, priority, risk, or the cost of doing nothing.
Do not argue with the objection. Change the angle of the conversation.
Useful language
“The bigger question is…”
“The way I’d look at it is…”
“It may help to look at this in terms of…”
“Instead of looking only at the cost, I’d also consider…”
“The real issue is whether this solves the problem you described.”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“No, it’s not expensive because we have many features.”
Stronger
“I understand the price concern. The bigger question is whether the current problem is costing you more than the solution would.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“We already have another provider.”
Model response
“That makes sense. The bigger question may be whether your current setup is still giving you the results your team needs.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
“It’s too expensive.”
Task: Reframe from price to value, problem, impact, or outcome.
“The bigger question is whether the cost of the problem is higher than the cost of solving it.”
“Instead of looking only at price, I’d look at what this helps you improve or avoid.”
“We already have another provider.”
Task: Reframe from provider comparison to business result.
“Our current provider is cheaper.”
Challenge: The client makes a direct cost comparison. Reframe without dismissing their point.
Quick Recap
A reframe helps you avoid defending every detail.
“The bigger question is…”
Skill 5 of 6

Respond With Value

Key Move: Connect your answer back to the client’s problem, desired result, or business impact.
Why this matters
A weak objection response explains features. A stronger response explains why the solution matters. The client needs a reason to believe the value is worth the cost, time, or effort.
Do not just explain what it is. Explain why it matters.
Useful language
“The value here is…”
“This matters because…”
“Based on what you described, this would help by…”
“The main benefit is not just ___, but ___.”
“This is designed to reduce / improve / prevent…”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“We have good support and many features.”
Stronger
“The value is that your team would spend less time managing this manually, which means fewer delays and more consistent follow-up.”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“I don’t see why this is worth changing now.”
Model response
“The value is that it helps your team reduce manual follow-up and avoid delays that are already costing time each week.”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
Feature: “We provide weekly reporting.”
Task: Turn this feature into a value statement. Start with “The value of weekly reporting is that…”
“I’m not sure this is worth it.”
Task: Respond by connecting the solution to a problem or result.

Useful structure: “The value depends on ___. If the main issue is ___, then this helps by ___.”
“Can you explain that in simple terms?”
Challenge: The client asks for simplicity. Restate the value without jargon or over-explaining.
Quick Recap
Value is not just what the product does. Value is what changes for the client.
“The value here is…”
Skill 6 of 6

Move the Conversation Forward

Key Move: End the objection response with a next step.
Why this matters
A good objection response should not only explain. It should lead somewhere. If the conversation ends with “Okay, I’ll think about it,” momentum can disappear.
Do not let the objection become the end of the conversation.
Useful language
“The next logical step would be…”
“Would it make sense to…?”
“What would you need to see before deciding?”
“Can we agree on what you’d need to feel comfortable moving forward?”
“Would it be useful to schedule a brief follow-up?”
Weak → Stronger
Weak
“Okay, no problem. Let me know.”
Stronger
“That makes sense. What would you need to review before deciding whether this is worth moving forward?”
See it in action
A short model response using this skill.
Client
“Send me some information and I’ll think about it.”
Model response
“Of course. I can send that over. Would it also make sense to schedule a short follow-up so we can review any questions together?”
Controlled
Applied
Make It More Realistic
“I understand the price concern. The value depends on how much time and cost this problem is creating for your team.”
Task: Add a next step to this response.
“Would it make sense to compare this with your current process?”
“What would you need to see before deciding?”
“Would a short follow-up be useful after you’ve reviewed the numbers?”
“Can you just send me more information?”
Task: Avoid a dead follow-up. Create a clearer, more specific next step.
“I don’t want another meeting unless there’s a clear reason.”
Challenge: The client resists a next step. Propose one that gives them a specific reason to say yes.
Quick Recap
The goal is not just to answer. The goal is to keep the conversation moving.
“What would you need to see to feel comfortable moving forward?”

See the five steps as a realistic turn-by-turn exchange

“We already use another provider.”
You Acknowledge + Clarify
“That makes sense. Can I ask whether your current provider is fully solving the issue, or whether there are still gaps you want to improve?”
Client
“They’re fine, but there are still some delays.”
You Reframe
“Then the question may not be whether you already have a provider, but whether the current setup is giving you the result you need.”
Client

You Respond + Move Forward
“If there is still a gap, it may be worth comparing where we approach it differently. Would it be useful to look at that comparison briefly?”

Practice Upgrade

Objection Challenge Bank

Once the response structure is clear, try the same prompt with one extra difficulty. Add one challenge at a time.

Tap any card to expand it.

Short Answers
“Maybe. I’m not sure.”

Do not overtalk. Ask one focused clarification question and wait.

Interruption
“Yes, but that still sounds expensive.”

Acknowledge the interruption, answer briefly, then return to value.

Time Limit
You only have 30 seconds to respond.

Use only three moves: acknowledge, clarify, suggest a next step.

Skeptical Tone
“I’ve heard that before.”

Do not argue. Ask what would make the claim more credible or specific.

Price Too Early
“How much does it cost?” — before understanding the value.

Answer professionally, but first clarify scope, need, or expected outcome.

Provider Comparison
“Our current provider already does this.”

Do not attack the provider. Ask what works, what is missing, and where improvement matters.

Dead Follow-Up Risk
“Just send me something.”

Clarify what information would help, then attach a clear reason for the follow-up.

Decision Delay
“Let me think about it and get back to you.”

Ask what they need to think through, then agree a specific next step or review point.

Standard

What Good Looks Like

A strong objection response should feel calm, clear, and useful. There is no single perfect answer — but strong objection handling usually includes a few clear qualities.

1
Calm opening
“I understand why that would be a concern.” — The speaker does not panic, apologise too much, or defend immediately.
2
Clarification
“Can I clarify whether the concern is cost, timing, or expected return?” — The speaker checks what the objection really means.
3
Value connection
“The value here is that it helps your team make the decision with clearer criteria and less uncertainty.”
4
Clear next step
“Would it make sense to compare this with your current process?” — The response keeps the conversation moving.
5
Professional tone
The speaker sounds steady, not defensive or desperate.
Strong response checklist

A strong response usually does at least three of these:

acknowledges the concern
asks a clarifying question
avoids over-apologising
explains value clearly
stays calm under pushback
suggests a next step
Example Strong Exchange
Client
“I need to think about it.”
You Clarify
“Of course. Can I ask what part you need to think through — the cost, the timing, or whether it fits your current priorities?”
Client
“Mostly whether it fits our priorities right now.”
You Value
“That makes sense. Then I should send you something focused on priority and business impact, not just general information.”
Client
“Yes, that would be more useful.”
You Move Forward
“If it makes sense, after you’ve reviewed that, we can decide whether a short follow-up conversation is worth scheduling.”
